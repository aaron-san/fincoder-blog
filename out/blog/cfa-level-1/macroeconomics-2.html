CFA® Level 1: Macroconomics (Part 1)

Macroeconomics

Monopolistic Competition and Oligopoly

Monopolistic Competition

  • A market in which there are many similar, but slightly differentiated products which are close substitutes. Each firm faces a downward sloping demand curve that shifts downward in the long-run until Price equals ATC (economic profit = 0)

Oligopoly

  • A market in which there are a few interdependent firms with significant barriers to entry (due to economies of scale)
  • Oligopolistic firms sometimes form cartels in which they agree to restrict production and raise prices

Factors of Production

Derived Demand

  • Demand for a factor of production driven by the demand for a product
  • Demand for factors of production are derived demands

Monopsony

A market in which there is a single buyer e.g. auto manufacturer as the single employer (buyer of labor)

Factors of Production

  1. Land
  2. Labor
  3. Capital
  4. Entrepreneurship

Marginal Revenue Product (MRP)

  • The additional revenue from an additional unit of the factor of production

  • The marginal revenue product curve is the firm's short-run demand curve for the productive input

  • A firm will use more units of input until the input's marginal revenue product is equal to market price

  • The demand for labor depends on the marginal revenue product of labor

  • The supply of labor faces two contradictory forces - (substitution effect) and (income effect).

  • The quantity of labor increases as the wage rate increase but eventually bends backwords as workers value leisure more than work (income effect).

At the level of labor input where marginal product is greatest, marginal cost is at its minimum. Marginal cost then increases as more labor input is added. Up to the point where average product is greatest, average variable cost decreases as labor input increases.

Industries that are labor intensive will be more sensitive to the wage rate and will adjust employment more for a given change in the wage rate.

Labor responds to real wages, net of inflation and payroll taxes

Renewable (re-useable)Natural Resource Supply of renewable resources is fixed (perfectly in-elastic) Supply is called "known stock" Example: land

Non-renewable Natural Resource Supply is pefectly elastic at a price that equals the present value of its future price Hotelling principle: If resource prices are expected to increase by less than the risk-free rate, suppliers will increase current production and invest proceeds at the risk-free rate

Example: oil

Historical Funding Sources for Private Business in U.S. 1.) Loans (60%) 2.) Bonds (30%) 3.) Equities (2%)

Opportunity Cost The amount necessary to bid a factor of production away from its next-highest-valued alternative use

Economic Rent The income above the opportunity cost of capital earned by an owner of a factor of production

Aggregate Supply (AS) Short-run Aggregate Supply (SAS) Workers' wage demands are constant Increase in expected future inflation increases higher wage demands, Marginal Cost of production increases, and short-run aggregate supply shifts leftward.

Long-run Aggregate Supply (LAS) Expected inflation equals actual inflation Full employment (zero cyclical unemployment) Potential real output (GDP) of economy

Determinants of Aggregate Supply (LAS) Quantity of labor Quantity of capital Technology

Aggregate Demand (AD) Aggregate Expenditures (AE) = C+I+G+NX Aggregate Demand (AD) = relationship between aggregate expenditures (AE = C+I+G+NX) and the price level (P) Prices increase → Interest rates increase: When the price level rises, individuals and businesses have less real money and must borrow, therefore the demand for loanable funds increases, thereby increasing the interest rate.

Wealth effect - At higher price levels, individuals and businesses have less wealth and therefore begin to spend less Substitution effect - At higher price levels, the demand for loanable funds increases as individuals need more to maintain real spending levels, causing an increase in interest rates. Consequently, higher interest rates reduce current spending as individuals and businesses save at higher opportunity costs of spending (saving is more valuable than spending)

Shifts in Aggregrate Demand Curve Shifts in expectations about inflation, income and future profits Monetary and Fiscal policy World economy

Types of Unemployment Frictional Unemployment (permanent) - unemployment arrising from normal turnover - entering and leaving the labor force and from ongoing cration and destruction of jobs.

Structural Unemployment (long-term) - unemployment caused by changes in technology that force workers to improve skills. Cyclical Unemployment - unemployment caused by the business cycle.

Full Employment Situation when there is no cyclical unemployment Produces the natural unemployment rate

Potential GDP GDP output at full employment

Business Cycle Growth, inflation, and business cycles arise from the relentless increases in potential GDP, faster (on the average) increases in aggregate demand, and fluctuations in the pace of aggregate demand growth

The cyclical nature of Real GDP around potential GDP that is caused by the stickiness of money wage rates and its tendency to adjust slower than the price level

An increase in the quantity of money at full employment will reduce interest rates in the short run, which would increase short-run aggregate demand above full employment. This causes wage demands to increase, which in turn reduces short-run aggregate suply. In the long run, real GDP reverts to its full-employment level with an increase in price level equal to the percentage increase in the quantity of money.

Workers lose the buying power of their wages and demand more from their employers, causing an increase in each firm's Marginal Cost curve and a leftward shift in the Short-run aggregate supply curve, which further increases the price level, but brings GDP back to its long-term equilibrium level (potential GDP - GDP with zero cyclical unemployment)

The economy "overheats" if the real GDP grows much faster than the money wage rate, requiring a sharp correction as workers demand more wages and real output raises prices and reduces output to accomadate the higher wage costs.

Stagflation (recession and inflation) occurs when short-run aggregate supply decreases, causing higher prices and less output (GDP)

Deflation A persistent decline in prices. Consumers respond by delaying consumption which reduces Aggregate Demand and Real GDP.

Mainstream Business Cycle Theory Cause of business cycle is due to variations in the growth of Aggregate Demand Potential GDP grows at a steady rate Money wage rate is sticky

Real Business Cycle Theory (newest) The real business cycle is caused by changes in technology that bring fluctuations in the growth rate of Productivity Changes in technology will affect: 1.) Investment demand 2.) Demand for labor

GDP Gap GDP Gap = Potential GDP - Real GDP Recessionary Gap: Real GDP < potential GDP Inflationary Gap: Real GDP > potential GDP

Short-Run Phillips Curve Inflation and unemployment curve Holds expected inflation and natural unemployment rate constant A decrease in expected inflation shifts short-run Phillips curve down If inflation rises above its expected rate, unemployment falls below its natural rate

Long-Run Phillips Curve Vertical line at the natural unemployment rate An increase in the natural unemployment rate shifts LRPC and SRPC rightward

Macroeconomic Schools of Thought

Classical Economy is self-regulating and always at full employment Technological change is most significant influence on AggDem and AggSupp

Keynsian Expectations ("Animal Spirits") are the most significant influence on aggregate demand Wage rate is sticky in the downward direction Business cycles can be stabilized best by use of Fiscal and Monetary policy Keynesian economists believe that a market economy, if left alone, is unstable and likely to experience prolonged recessions. As a result, governments should run budget deficits during a recession in order to stimulate demand and direct the economy back to full employment.

Monetarist The quantity of money is the most significant influence on aggregate demand Business cycles can be stabilized best by maintaining money supply growth steady and predictable The money wage rate is sticky

Supply-Side Economists Promote tax cuts to encourage workers to work more labor hours to increase potential GDP (supply)

Required Reserves = Demand Deposits * Required Reserve Ratio Excess Reserves = Actual Reserves - Required Researves

Federal Reserve System ("The FED") Central Bank ("Banks' bank") Regulates nation's depository institutions Controls the quantity of money

Goals of the Fed 1.) Stable prices (1-2% inflation : %ΔCPI) 2.) Full employment (zero cyclical unemployment) 3.) Moderate long-term interest rates 4.) Contribute to long-term real GDP growth

Federal funds rate The rate that banks charge eachother on overnight loans of reserves

Taxes on Interest Income (Savings) The interest rate that influences savers is the real after-tax interest rate. Since taxes depend on the nominal rate, the higher the inflation rate, the higher the taxes on interest income When a tax on savings is imposed, the supply of loanable funds curve shifts leftward, increasing the equiblibrium real rate and decreasing the equilibrium quantity of loanable funds.

Crowding-Out Effect A budget deficit shifts the supply of private loanable funds curve leftward, decreasing the equilibrium quantity of loanable funds and increasing the equilibrium real interest rate, reducing total Investment.

A budget surplus increases the supply of private loanable funds, decreasing the equilibrium real interest rate, increasing the equilibrium quantity of loanable funds, increasing total Investment.

Government stimulus in the form of spending more than the amount of tax revenues (budget deficit) dries up loanable funds to the business sector, increasing equilibrium real interest rates, and decreasing Investment.

Economic Stimulus (via Interest Rate Targeting) The Fed buys short-term Treasury Securities The Supply of reserves increases The Federal Funds rate decreases The money supply increases (money multiplier effect) General interest rates decrease (the powerful substitution effect) Saving is less attractive, consumption increases Capital expenditures are more attractive, Investment increases Domestic interest rates become less attractive, the domestic currency depreciates Domestic products are more affordable to foreign consumers, increasing exports and decreasing imports, net exports increases Aggregate demand (real GDP, C+I+G+EX, assuming the price level does not change) increases Real GDP increases, the price level rises Labor wages adjust to inflation and the Marginal Cost of Production increases, shifting the Short-run Aggregate Supply curve leftward until it intersects the new Aggregate Demand at the potential level of real GDP

Short-Term Rates vs. Long-Term Rates Short-term debt instruments are close substitutes Short-term interest rates and Long-term interest rates are only loosely linked Long-term interest rates influence consumer and business economic decisions If a decrease in short-term interest rates is triggered by inflationary money supply increases, long-term interest rates will incorporate the additional expected inflation, and decrease disproportionately

If the central bank decreases the money supply unexpectedly, real wages increase because actual inflation is less than expected inflation. This causes a short-run decrease in aggregate supply (more expensive labor), decreasing real GDP growth and increasing the unemployment rate

If inflation is expected, the fluctuations in real GDP that accompany demand-pull and cost-push inflation don't occur. Instead, inflation proceeds as it does in the long run with real GDP equal to potential GDP and unemployment at its natural rate.
