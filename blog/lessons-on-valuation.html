Lessons on Valuation

It is possible to value almost anything. From stocks and bonds to real estate and businesses, valuation is a critical skill for investors and financial professionals. This article describes some of the various valuation methods used.

Price vs. Value

Download

In efficient and unrestricted markets, the price of an asset cannot deviate in the long term from its intrinsic value. Historically, asset prices have shown the capacity for self-correction. This capacity for self-correction is driven by market participants who identify mispriced assets and act to exploit these opportunities, thereby pushing prices back toward their intrinsic values.

How long is the long-run? Unfortunately, nobody knows for sure and as John Maynard Keynes once said, "In the long run, we're all dead." The point is that while prices can and do deviate from intrinsic value for extended periods of time, in the long-run they tend to converge.

Perceptions of Value

Value is subjective and can vary significantly among different investors based on their individual perspectives, risk tolerances, and investment horizons. For example, growth investors may value a company based on its future earnings potential, while value investors may focus on current assets and cash flows.

Common Valuation Methods

Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Analysis

DCF analysis estimates the present value of an asset based on its expected future cash flows, discounted back to their present value using a discount rate that reflects the asset's risk.

Comparable Company Analysis (CCA)

CCA involves evaluating similar companies in the same industry to determine a fair value for the target company based on valuation multiples such as Price-to-Earnings (P/E) or Enterprise Value-to-EBITDA (EV/EBITDA).

Precedent Transactions Analysis

This method looks at historical transactions of similar companies to establish valuation benchmarks based on the prices paid in those deals.

Asset-Based Valuation

Asset-based valuation calculates a company's value based on the net asset value (NAV) of its underlying assets, subtracting liabilities from total assets.
